Gems

Black Jade

  • Jade is an umbrella term for two different types of decorative rocks used for jewelry or ornaments. Jade is often referred to by either of two different silicate mineral names: nephrite (a silicate of calcium and magnesium in the amphibole group of minerals), or jadeite (a silicate of sodium and aluminum in the pyroxene group of minerals).

Gold

  • Gold is a chemical element; it has symbol Au (from Latin aurum) and atomic number 79. In its pure form, it is a bright, slightly orange-yellow, dense, soft, malleable, and ductile metal.

Malaya Garnet

  • Malaia garnet or Malaya garnet is a gemological varietal name for light to dark slightly pinkish orange, reddish orange, or yellowish orange garnet, that are of a mixture within the pyralspite series pyrope, almandine, and spessartine with a little calcium. The name Malaia is translated from Swahili to mean, prostitute. It is found in east Africa, in the Umba Valley bordering Tanzania and Kenya.

Tanzanite (Uncooked)

  • Tanzanite is the blue and violet variety of the mineral zoisite (a calcium aluminium hydroxyl sorosilicate), caused by small amounts of vanadium. Tanzanite belongs to the epidote mineral group. Tanzanite is only found in Simanjiro District of Manyara Region in Tanzania.

Tanzanite (Cooked)

  • See above, tanzanite forms as a brownish crystal and is trichroic, which means it shows three colours – brown, blue and violet – concurrently. Heating, either underground naturally by metamorphic processes, or artificially, removes the brown or burgundy colour component to produce a stronger violet-blue color and makes the stone "dichroic", which means it only reflects blue and violet..

Pearl

  • A pearl is a hard, glistening object produced within the soft tissue (specifically the mantle) of a living shelled mollusk or another animal, such as fossil conulariids. Just like the shell of a mollusk, a pearl is composed of calcium carbonate (mainly aragonite or a mixture of aragonite and calcite) in minute crystalline form, which has deposited in concentric layers.

Sapphire

  • Sapphire is a precious gemstone, a variety of the mineral corundum, consisting of aluminium oxide with trace amounts of elements such as iron, titanium, cobalt, lead, chromium, vanadium, magnesium, boron, and silicon. The name sapphire is derived from the Greek word for lapis lazuli.

Topaz

  • Topaz is a silicate mineral made of aluminum and fluorine. It is used as a gemstone in jewelry and other adornments. Common topaz in its natural state is colorless, though trace element impurities can make it pale blue or golden brown to yellow-orange. Topaz is often treated with heat or radiation to make it a deep blue, reddish-orange, pale green, pink, or purple.

Ruby

  • Ruby is a pinkish red to blood-red colored gemstone, a variety of the mineral corundum (aluminium oxide). The color is due to the element chromium.

Ruby

  • Ruby is a pinkish red to blood-red colored gemstone, a variety of the mineral corundum (aluminium oxide). The color is due to the element chromium.

Garnet

  • Garnets are a group of silicate minerals that have been used since the Bronze Age as gemstones and abrasives.

Aquamarine

  • Aquamarine is a pale-blue to light-green variety of the beryl family, with its name relating to water and sea.

Diamond

  • Diamond is a solid form of the element carbon with its atoms arranged in a crystal structure called diamond cubic.

Emeralds

  • Emerald is a gemstone and a variety of the mineral beryl colored green by trace amounts of chromium or sometimes vanadium.

Chrysoprase

  • Chrysoprase, chrysophrase or chrysoprasus is a gemstone variety of chalcedony (a cryptocrystalline form of silica) that contains small quantities of nickel. Its color is normally apple-green, but varies from turquoise-like cyan to deep green. The darker varieties of chrysoprase are also referred to as prase.

Coral

  • An organic gemstone formed from the hardened skeletons of marine coral polyps, prized for its vibrant red, pink, and orange hues. It has been used in jewelry and talismans for centuries, often symbolizing protection and vitality.

Jet

  • Jet is a lignite, a fossilized form of wood created under extreme pressure over millions of years, chemically similar to coal but compacted into a gemstone. It consists primarily of carbon with traces of organic compounds, giving it a lightweight yet durable nature suitable for carving and polishing.

Chrome Tourmaline

  • Chrome tourmaline is a crystalline boron silicate mineral that gains its intense green coloration from trace amounts of chromium and vanadium. Its trigonal crystal structure and high refractive index contribute to its brilliance and make it a distinct gemological variety of tourmaline.

Amethyst

  • Amethyst is a purple quartz variant whose color arises from iron impurities and natural radiation exposure during its formation. Its crystal lattice is hexagonal, and it exhibits piezoelectric properties, making it both aesthetically valued and scientifically intriguing.

Jade

  • Jade encompasses two mineral species: jadeite, a pyroxene rich in aluminum, and nephrite, an amphibole mineral. Both are highly durable, formed under metamorphic conditions, and prized for their microcrystalline interlocking structure that enables intricate carvings.

Agate

  • Agate is a microcrystalline variety of quartz characterized by its concentric banding formed from silica deposition in cavities of volcanic rocks. Its durability and intricate patterns result from varying chemical compositions and crystallization rates.

Herkimer 'Diamond' Quartz

  • Herkimer quartz crystals are naturally double-terminated quartz specimens, notable for their high clarity and unique growth conditions in dolomite rock formations. These crystals are formed in pockets within the rock, free from attachment to host material, resulting in their characteristic shape.

Watermelon Tourmaline

  • Watermelon tourmaline features color zoning due to variations in trace element concentrations during crystallization, with manganese causing pink hues and iron creating green. Its radial growth patterns within a trigonal system are a key feature in mineralogical studies.

Tsavorite

  • Tsavorite is a calcium-aluminum silicate garnet colored by vanadium or chromium, forming under high-pressure metamorphic conditions. Its high refractive index and dispersion properties give it remarkable brilliance, rivaling that of more commonly known gemstones.

Sphene

  • Sphene, or titanite, is a calcium titanium silicate mineral known for its high dispersion, which creates fire-like flashes of color. Its monoclinic crystal structure and pleochroism contribute to its gemological significance and scientific interest.

Picture Agate

  • Picture agate forms through the deposition of silica-rich solutions in layers, creating natural patterns resembling images or landscapes. The inclusions of iron and manganese oxides influence the colors and designs within the quartz matrix.

Fire Agate

  • Fire agate's iridescence arises from thin layers of limonite or hematite embedded within its microcrystalline quartz structure. These layers diffract light, producing the gemstone's characteristic fiery play of colors.

Moss Agate

  • Moss agate contains dendritic inclusions of manganese or iron that form plant-like patterns within the chalcedony matrix. These inclusions are unrelated to organic material but mimic the appearance of moss or foliage.

Agate

  • Agate is a microcrystalline variety of quartz characterized by its concentric banding formed from silica deposition in cavities of volcanic rocks. Its durability and intricate patterns result from varying chemical compositions and crystallization rates.

Tiger Eye

  • Tiger eye is a quartz variety displaying chatoyancy due to fibrous inclusions of crocidolite asbestos that are replaced by silica over time. This process, known as pseudomorphism, creates its silky luster and banded appearance.

Chrysoprase

  • Chrysoprase, chrysophrase or chrysoprasus is a gemstone variety of chalcedony (a cryptocrystalline form of silica) that contains small quantities of nickel. Its color is normally apple-green, but varies from turquoise-like cyan to deep green. The darker varieties of chrysoprase are also referred to as prase.

Blue Lace Agate

  • Blue lace agate exhibits banded patterns of silica and trace minerals, creating its soft blue and white appearance. Its layers are formed through rhythmic silica deposition in volcanic host rocks.

Polished Fossils

  • A concretion of fossilized shells polished into a gemstone.

Dinosaur Bone

  • Gembone, also known as agatized dinosaur bone, is a unique and rare form of fossilized dinosaur bone that has been transformed into colorful and durable gemstone material through a process of mineral replacement.

Carnelian

  • Carnelian is a chalcedony variety with iron oxide inclusions that give it an orange to reddish-brown color. Its cryptocrystalline structure ensures durability and translucency, making it historically significant for carvings and seals.

Sodalite

  • Sodalite is a tectosilicate mineral primarily composed of sodium, aluminum, and chlorine, often featuring a deep blue coloration due to its crystalline structure. It forms in silica-poor environments, such as igneous rocks like nepheline syenite.

Lapis Lazuli

  • Lapis lazuli is a metamorphic rock composed of lazurite, calcite, and pyrite, valued for its intense blue color and metallic inclusions. The sulfur content in lazurite is responsible for its characteristic hue.

Abalone Shell

  • Abalone shell is composed of aragonite, a calcium carbonate mineral arranged in a microscopic, layered nacre structure. This arrangement causes interference and diffraction of light, producing its iridescent sheen.

Labradorite

  • Labradorite is a feldspar mineral displaying labradorescence, an optical effect caused by light scattering within its thin, interlocking layers. This plagioclase feldspar forms in igneous rocks, such as basalt and gabbro.

Opal

  • Opal is an amorphous form of silica with a high water content, exhibiting play-of-color due to its internal arrangement of silica spheres. This phenomenon arises from the diffraction and interference of light waves.

Turquoise

  • Turquoise is a hydrated copper aluminum phosphate mineral known for its opaque blue to green coloration. Its formation occurs in arid environments through the weathering of copper-bearing rocks.

Shaktokite

  • (Verify) Shattuckite is a secondary copper silicate mineral forming in oxidized zones of copper deposits. Its vibrant blue color and fibrous aggregates are indicative of its mineralogical class.

Unakite

  • Unakite is a metamorphic rock composed primarily of epidote, orthoclase feldspar, and quartz, resulting in its green and pink coloration. It forms through the alteration of granite by hydrothermal activity.

Malachite

  • Malachite is a basic copper carbonate mineral forming in the oxidized zones of copper ore deposits. Its concentric banding and rich green hues result from its crystalline and microcrystalline structures.

Pink Druzy

  • Druzy (also spelled druse or drusy) is sets of tiny crystals of minerals that form on the surface of another stone. These crystal intergrowths or clusters can be formed by many different minerals.

Moonstone

  • Moonstone is a potassium feldspar exhibiting adularescence, a glow caused by light diffraction within its orthoclase and albite layers. This phenomenon results from its crystalline structure and lamellar twinning.

Green Jasper

  • Green jasper is a dense, opaque variety of microcrystalline quartz colored by iron silicates or chlorite inclusions. Its uniform texture and durability have made it a favored material for carvings and tools throughout history.
